## Motivation

The [cosine paradox](/research/cosine_paradox) showed that pairwise similarity hides contradiction. The complementary question is: when two embeddings *do* encode a contradiction, can we recover the *direction* along which they disagree?

A clean candidate: the Householder reflection that maps one embedding to the other.

## The Householder construction

For two unit vectors $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ and $\hat{\mathbf{v}}$ with $\hat{\mathbf{u}} \neq \hat{\mathbf{v}}$, define

$$
\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{\hat{\mathbf{u}} - \hat{\mathbf{v}}}{\lVert \hat{\mathbf{u}} - \hat{\mathbf{v}} \rVert}.
$$

The Householder reflection across the hyperplane orthogonal to $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is

$$
H = I - 2\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}\hat{\mathbf{n}}^{\!\top},
$$

with $H\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \hat{\mathbf{v}}$. The vector $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is the **contradiction direction** — the axis we have to flip the sentence across to turn one claim into its opposite.

## Why this is the right object

- **It is unique up to sign.** The reflection that maps $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ to $\hat{\mathbf{v}}$ is determined; its mirror plane is a single hyperplane.
- **It is interpretable.** Decoding the nearest concept words to $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ surfaces the contested axis. In the capital-flow example from the cosine paradox paper, the top decode is "exploitation / enrichment / direction-of-value-flow." The axis names itself.
- **It composes.** A long argument is many sentence pairs; the average $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ across pairs is a reading of the argument's principal contested axis.

## Sanity checks

We ran Householder recovery on 50 hand-curated contradiction pairs from political philosophy texts (left/right framings of the same issue). For each pair, we computed $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ and found its top-5 nearest concept words in the encoder's vocabulary. Three independent annotators rated whether the surfaced concept matched the contested axis they would name.

| Metric                                  | Value |
|------------------------------------------|-------|
| Pairs where ≥2 of 3 annotators agreed   | 41/50 |
| Mean cosine of $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ to annotator-supplied axis | 0.63 |
| Pairs with zero recoverable axis        | 4/50  |

The four failure cases were all sentence pairs whose disagreement was structural (one denied a presupposition the other relied on) rather than directional.

## How this feeds the engine

Layer 4 (embedding geometry) replaces "high cosine = similar" with a richer triple: **(topic similarity, contradiction direction, contradiction magnitude)**. The contradiction direction enters the composite score by reducing the weight of layered claims that all flip across the *same* axis — a long argument that contradicts itself in one direction is more incoherent than one that contradicts itself in many directions, because it concentrates the failure mode.

## Limits

- Reflection assumes a single contested axis between two claims. Multi-axis disagreement is currently approximated by sequential reflections; the right object there is a higher-dimensional rotation, not a reflection.
- The annotator agreement rate is moderate (82% on a small sample). The procedure is not a finished detector.
- Like every other layer, this contributes to a composite coherence score whose predictive validity is unproven. The reflection is a structural diagnostic, not a forecast.
