## The setup

Take two sentences that, on their plain semantic content, contradict each other:

> *S₁:* "Capital flows from labor to ownership through extracted surplus."
> *S₂:* "Capital flows from ownership to labor through delivered value."

Embed both with a strong sentence encoder (we used `all-mpnet-base-v2`). Compute the cosine similarity:

$$
\cos(\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2) = \frac{\mathbf{v}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v}_2}{\lVert \mathbf{v}_1 \rVert \, \lVert \mathbf{v}_2 \rVert}.
$$

The result is consistently **high** — typically above 0.85 — even though one sentence is the analytical inverse of the other.

## Why this is paradoxical

A naive reader expects high cosine similarity to mean "these sentences agree" and low cosine similarity to mean "these sentences disagree." The cosine paradox is that the function trained on next-sentence and paraphrase tasks treats *topic* and *vocabulary* as the dominant axes of variation. Two sentences that share topic and vocabulary land near each other regardless of whether they make opposite claims along that topic's most important axis.

The geometry: the contradiction lives in a small subspace defined by the contested axis (here, the direction of capital flow). The embedding has thousands of dimensions; the contested axis is one of them. Cosine averages over all dimensions, so a small directional disagreement is dwarfed by a large topical agreement.

## Empirical evidence

We constructed 200 sentence pairs with the structure "X does Y to Z" / "Z does Y to X" and 200 pairs with the structure "X does Y to Z" / "X does Y to W" (a topic-shifted control). Mean cosine similarity:

| Pair type                        | Mean cos | Interpretation       |
|----------------------------------|----------|----------------------|
| Direction-flipped (contradicts)  | 0.871    | "very similar"       |
| Topic-shifted (does not contradict) | 0.512    | "moderately similar" |

A cosine-only classifier would rate the contradicting pair as more similar than the non-contradicting one. This is not a bug in the encoder — it correctly reflects topical and vocabulary overlap. It is a bug in our use of cosine as a contradiction signal.

## Implication for coherence scoring

Embedding-geometry analysis (Layer 4 of the engine) cannot rely on raw cosine similarity for contradiction detection. We need a signal that separates the **direction** of disagreement from the topical baseline. The companion paper, [Contradiction Direction](/research/contradiction_direction), develops one such signal using Householder reflections.

## Limits

- Sample size is small (n = 400 synthetic pairs). Replication on larger natural-text corpora is pending.
- We have not characterised the encoder dependence — the magnitude of the paradox likely shifts with model family and training objective.
- The "paradox" framing is rhetorical. The behaviour is fully consistent with what cosine similarity is *defined* to measure; the paradox is that practitioners frequently treat it as something it is not.
- This paper does not show that any composite coherence score has predictive validity for downstream outcomes. That is a separate question.
